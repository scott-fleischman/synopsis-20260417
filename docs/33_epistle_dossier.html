These are the cases traditional scholarship debates most; the dossier separates formula risk from retrieval strength so each can be weighted independently. The 10-case targeted set below also includes Did 9–10 / Lord's Prayer, 1 Pet 2:12 / Matt 5:16, and others.

Epistle-to-Gospel evidence is assessed case by case, not corpus-wide. Each dossier separates three confidence axes: formula risk (how much of the match is liturgical/scriptural/proverbial), automatic retrieval support (how strong the lexical signal is), and philological case strength (what a qualitative reading of the pair concludes).

Why three axes, not one score. A high retrieval score on 1 Cor 11:24 ~ Luke 22:19 (six-token Greek run) is not evidence of direct Gospel dependence — it reflects the shared Eucharistic formula, which is why its formula risk is high. The 1 Tim 5:18 ~ Luke 10:7 parallel has a shorter five-token run but low formula risk, so its philological case strength rises to high and the best-current-model takes the direct-copying hypothesis seriously. The ranked-candidate top 500 also reports these axes: 317 low-risk, 166 high-risk, 17 medium-risk candidates. A 160-row stratified sample (see panel below) adds a machine-audit class to each candidate: 41 strong low-formula, 18 moderate, 59 uncertain, 26 scriptural-formula shared-source, 13 targeted known, 3 high-formula needing manual review — triage that flags which cases are worth philological reading first.
