Mark ↔ Luke direct

The missing Synoptic control. If Mark is Matthew's narrative source, the same test has to work for Luke, or the Markan-priority claim is only half-supported. This dataset supplies it. The pipeline is the same as Mark↔Matthew — full verse-matrix, dynamic-programming chain, loose/tight block segmentation, direction-burden ledger, threshold sensitivity — but with one text-critical default: Mark 16:9–20 is excluded from the primary chain by default and retained as a sensitivity condition. That choice matters because including the long ending adds exactly one chain pair, which would otherwise become evidence for a textual artifact.

What this page answers. Does Luke's relationship to Mark look like Matthew's? Partly. Luke has 183 primary-chain pairs versus Matthew's 385 — still a monotonic backbone, but narrower. Between the two direct-use models, Mark-prior (254.6) is lighter than Luke-prior (303.2). The unpenalized shared-tradition figure (164.1) looks cheaper than either direction — but shared tradition still has to account for the narrative order, cluster density, and bridge sequences it produces, which the unpenalized baseline does not charge it for. Under the 18c order-retention penalty (moderate weights 0.75/1.0/1.0) shared tradition adjusts to 385.6, and Mark-prior becomes the lowest-burden model overall (Mark-prior wins in 211 of 245 grid cells). Read that as: Luke plausibly uses Mark or a Mark-like source for the common backbone; Lukan expansions (travel narrative, infancy, resurrection) sit outside Markan sequence and leave real headroom for additional sources, but shared tradition is not by itself cheaper than Mark-prior once order-retention is priced.

Three-way direction burden

Mark↔Luke does not collapse neatly onto two hypotheses — a shared-tradition model is a live third option. The bars show the total operational strain each hypothesis must explain, summed across all 35 loose blocks. Lower is cheaper. A single lowest does not decide the direction; the asymmetry and the shape of the burden across blocks does.
Read the ledger honestly. The unpenalized three-way ledger above shows shared tradition (164.1) cheapest. But an unpenalized shared-tradition model is not a like-for-like comparison: it is not charged for the narrative order and cluster density that the shared material must still produce in Luke. The 18c patch (12_mark_luke_direct_burden_reassessment.yaml) sweeps an explicit order-retention/cluster-density/narrative-bridge penalty across a 245-cell weight grid. At moderate weights (0.75 / 1.0 / 1.0) the added shared-tradition penalty is 221.47, adjusting shared tradition to 385.6, and Mark-prior (254.6) becomes the lowest-burden model. Mark-prior wins in 211 of 245 grid combinations; shared-tradition-adjusted wins in 34. The honest public wording is therefore: Mark-prior is the lighter direct-use hypothesis, and — once shared tradition is charged a fair order-retention cost — Mark-prior is also the lowest-burden model overall. The Mark-Luke data alone still does not prove that Luke used canonical Mark rather than a Mark-like written source. See AUTHORITATIVE_NUMBERS.yaml#mark_luke and §7 of METHODS.md.

Secondary echoes

140 non-chain verse pairs with score ≥ 0.22. These are local near-matches the chain declined to bind (usually because a better match exists elsewhere for one side). Treat them as displaced or reused material, not as chain evidence. The strongest — Mark 1:2 ~ Luke 7:27 ("Behold I send my messenger") — sits far off the backbone because Luke places the quotation in the John-the-Baptist discourse rather than the prologue.
