Gospel Dependence & Early Christian Intertextuality Workbench

A layered, auditable investigation of literary dependence across the four canonical Gospels, the Gospel of Thomas, the New Testament letters, and a select inventory of early-Christian apocrypha. Built from the SBLGNT Greek text and apparatus, MorphGNT morphology, Coptic Scriptorium Thomas, and M. R. James's apocryphal inventory. Every layer is a separate evidence stream — retrieval scores, burden ledgers, sensitivity grids, negative-space controls — and every intermediate artifact is hash-audited at source.

This workbench does not settle the direction of dependence. It quantifies what each direction-hypothesis must explain, and flags shared Old Testament/liturgical formulas so they do not masquerade as Gospel dependence. The Conclusions page is the only surface that makes directional claims; every other page is neutral evidence. Conclusions revised 2026-04-19 from the revised_conclusions_review_20260419 review: nine-point model-aware ladder; Mark↔Luke promoted to its own rank with an explicit order-retention caveat; Farrer and Two-source named as the two live system-level competitors; minor agreements (118 rows, 21 high-strength) promoted to headline discriminator. Ten analysis packages feed the bundle — mark_matthew, matt_luke, matt_luke_double_masked, john_thomas_epistles_apocrypha, the 2026-04-18b high-priority supplement (direct Mark↔Luke · three John pairwise layers · canonical Gospel square · Thomas logion matrix · 60 epistle case dossiers · conclusion ↔ evidence navigation), the 2026-04-18c reassessment (Mark-Luke order-retention penalty grid), the morphgnt_lemma_supplement, the 2026-04-18 directional dossiers that answer direction by direction what every proposed dependence must explain, and the 2026-04-18 Synoptic Problem model atlas that raises the analysis from pairwise directions to system-level models (Markan priority · Two-source · Farrer · Griesbach · Augustinian · proto-Mark/network · oral · three John-direct · John-reverse · shared-anchor) with a 13-model scorecard, a 4×4 direction matrix, catalogs of minor agreements and displaced double-tradition, and a 102-pericope × per-model obligation browser. The evidence ordering below follows the supplement's priority-of-interpretation schedule: start with claims, then the Gospel square, then pairwise evidence, then direction-specific obligation dossiers, then the system-model atlas, then audit.

    Synoptic Problem model atlas · 13 system models, 5 explanatory areas

    Source: synoptic_problem_model_atlas_20260418/

    The directional dossiers cover pairs. The atlas lifts that analysis to system-level models: Markan priority, Two-source (Mark + Q-like sayings), Farrer (Mark + Matthew → Luke), Griesbach / Two-Gospel (Matthew → Luke → Mark), Augustinian, proto-Mark/network, oral-tradition-network, three John-direct models, John-reverse, and a shared-anchor model for John. Each of the 13 models is scored across five explanatory areas — Mark–Matthew, Mark–Luke, Matthew–Luke double tradition, triple-tradition minor agreements, and John anchors — with required direction lists, summed direction burdens where computable, main strength, and heaviest remaining burden. The scorecard and required-direction chips live on page 39; the compact 4×4 direction grid is on page 40; the 118 triple-tradition minor agreements and 109 double-tradition (order + echo) rows — the main discriminators between Q, Farrer, and Griesbach — are on page 41; the 102-unit, 1,945-obligation per-model pericope browser is on page 42.

    Seven research questions this workbench answers

    Each question has a distinct evidence shape. The backbone questions (Matthew/Mark, Matthew/Luke) can be posed as order-sensitive literary-dependence questions. The sayings questions (Thomas, Q, James) need a network model. The Johannine question needs anchor-level transformation analysis. Treating them with one pipeline would over-commit on the easy cases and under-detect on the hard ones.
