Mark ↔ Luke direct

The missing Synoptic control. If Mark is Matthew's narrative source, the same test has to work for Luke, or the Markan-priority claim is only half-supported. This dataset supplies it. The pipeline is the same as Mark↔Matthew — full verse-matrix, dynamic-programming chain, loose/tight block segmentation, direction-burden ledger, threshold sensitivity — but with one text-critical default: Mark 16:9–20 is excluded from the primary chain by default and retained as a sensitivity condition. That choice matters because including the long ending adds exactly one chain pair, which would otherwise become evidence for a textual artifact.

What this page answers. Does Luke's relationship to Mark look like Matthew's? Partly. Luke has 183 primary-chain pairs versus Matthew's 385 — still a monotonic backbone, but narrower. The three-way burden ledger shows Mark-prior (254.6) lighter than Luke-prior (303.2), but neither is crushing — and a shared-tradition model (164.1) is genuinely cheaper than either single-direction hypothesis. Read that as: Luke plausibly uses Mark or Mark-like material for the common backbone, but Lukan expansions (travel narrative, infancy, resurrection) sit outside Markan sequence and leave real headroom for additional sources.

Three-way direction burden

Mark↔Luke does not collapse neatly onto two hypotheses — a shared-tradition model is a live third option. The bars show the total operational strain each hypothesis must explain, summed across all 35 loose blocks. Lower is cheaper. A single lowest does not decide the direction; the asymmetry and the shape of the burden across blocks does.
Read the ledger honestly. The lowest burden belongs to the shared-tradition model (164.1), not to either directed hypothesis (Mark-prior 254.6; Luke-prior 303.2). The directed conclusion this page supports is therefore narrow: between the two single-source directions, Mark-prior is the lighter-burden model. The broader claim that Mark-prior is the overall lowest-cost explanation of Mark↔Luke is not supported by these numbers — shared oral/narrative tradition is cheaper than either direction alone. Primary chain pair explorer

183 Mark↔Luke pairs that form the monotonic backbone. Click any column header to sort. Score is the project's weighted-Jaccard + exact-run composite; run is the longest exact content-token sequence both verses share; LCS is the longest common content-token subsequence as a fraction of the shorter verse. Rows with Mark 16:9–20 are flagged but excluded from the default chain.

Secondary echoes

140 non-chain verse pairs with score ≥ 0.22. These are local near-matches the chain declined to bind (usually because a better match exists elsewhere for one side). Treat them as displaced or reused material, not as chain evidence. The strongest — Mark 1:2 ~ Luke 7:27 ("Behold I send my messenger") — sits far off the backbone because Luke places the quotation in the John-the-Baptist discourse rather than the prologue.
